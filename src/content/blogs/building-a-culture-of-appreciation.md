## "We Appreciate Our People" — Do You, Though?

Walk into any company and ask leadership if they value their employees, and you'll get a unanimous yes. Browse their Glassdoor page and the story might be different.

The gap between *saying* you appreciate people and *actually building a culture* where appreciation is felt daily is enormous — and most companies are stuck in that gap without knowing it.

---

## What a Genuine Culture of Appreciation Looks Like

A real appreciation culture has three defining characteristics:

### 1. It's Frequent, Not Just Annual

Recognition that happens once a year (annual awards, performance reviews) is better than nothing — but it's not a culture. A culture means employees regularly hear that their contributions matter.

Research from Gallup suggests that **the optimal recognition frequency is every 7 days** — at least a brief, sincere acknowledgement of what someone has done well.

### 2. It's Peer-to-Peer, Not Just Top-Down

When recognition only comes from managers, you've created a dependency. What happens when a manager is stretched, disengaged, or just not good at giving feedback?

The most resilient recognition cultures are **peer-driven** — where any employee can recognise any other employee, at any time. This is democratised appreciation, and it's far more scalable.

### 3. It's Specific and Behavioural

Vague praise ("Great team player!") has almost no impact. Specific, behavioural recognition ("You noticed that the client was confused before the demo and took 10 minutes to walk them through the platform — that's what client-first looks like") creates meaning and reinforces the values you actually want to see.

## The 5 Mistakes That Undermine Appreciation Cultures

### Mistake 1: Making It HR's Problem

Recognition shouldn't live in the HR department. It should be everyone's responsibility — from the CEO to a new hire. If only HR sends birthday messages and anniversary emails, you don't have a culture, you have an administrative process.

### Mistake 2: Tying Every Recognition to Performance

Not every recognition needs to be about hitting a metric. Sometimes you recognise someone for how they showed up for a struggling colleague, or how they handled a difficult conversation, or how they maintained quality during a chaotic sprint.

Human recognition needs to cover *character* and *effort*, not just outcomes.

### Mistake 3: One-Size-Fits-All Rewards

Some people are energised by public recognition. Others find it mortifying. Some love experiences; others prefer practical gifts. **Personalisation is respect**. Build systems that let employees receive recognition in ways that resonate with them.

### Mistake 4: Inconsistency

Few things damage a recognition culture faster than inconsistency. When some teams celebrate every win and others never do, it creates inequality. When some managers remember birthdays and others don't, it signals whose team matters more.

Build systems — tools, reminders, templates — that make consistency easy.

### Mistake 5: Not Measuring It

What gets measured gets managed. Track recognition frequency, peer participation rates, and employee sentiment. Use that data to identify gaps and intervene before they become retention problems.

## How to Start Building (or Rebuilding) Your Culture

You don't need a six-month transformation programme. Start here:

**Week 1:** Introduce a peer recognition channel (Slack, Teams, or a dedicated tool like WorkKudo)

**Week 2:** Educate managers on *specific* vs. *generic* recognition with concrete examples

**Week 3:** Set up automated milestone alerts so no birthday or anniversary is missed

**Month 2:** Launch a monthly "Kudos Spotlight" in your all-hands to publicly celebrate peer nominations

**Quarter 2:** Review your data — who's being recognised, who isn't, what's resonating

## The ROI of Appreciation

This isn't just a "nice to have." Companies with strong recognition cultures report:

- **31% lower voluntary turnover**
- **14% higher productivity** (Gallup)
- **2x the employee engagement scores** of companies without structured recognition

Appreciation isn't soft. It's one of the highest-leverage investments a company can make.
